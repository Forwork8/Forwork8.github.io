Communication & Self-Improvement

How to Win Friends and Influence People

by Dale Carnegie — Read in 2023

Year Read: 2023
Recommended

Learning Summary

What this book is really about

Written in 1936 and still the most widely read guide to human relations. Carnegie's thesis: people care more about being understood and appreciated than almost anything else. Master that, and most interpersonal problems dissolve.

As an engineer who primarily communicates through code, this forced me to think more carefully about how I present ideas in code reviews, proposals, and meetings.

"You can make more friends in two months by becoming interested in other people than you can in two years by trying to get other people interested in you."

Key Lessons

Ideas I took away

1
Criticism rarely works — appreciation does
People defend their beliefs when attacked. Change comes from making someone feel understood first. Never criticise without first establishing genuine appreciation.
2
Talk in terms of the other person's interests
When presenting an idea, frame it in terms of what it benefits the other person. Directly applicable to technical proposals, convincing a team to adopt a new architecture, or pitching to non-technical stakeholders.
3
Let others feel the idea is theirs
People champion ideas they helped shape. Asking questions instead of issuing instructions consistently produces better outcomes and more motivated collaborators.
4
Remember names — they are the sweetest sound in any language
Remembering and using someone's name signals respect and attention. This small habit builds professional goodwill disproportionate to its cost.

My Honest Opinion

Should you read it?

Adil's Verdict
Recommended

The ideas here are so foundational that most have been absorbed into management culture without attribution. Reading the original shows the reasoning behind each principle.

Recommended for any engineer who collaborates with humans. Which is all of us.

Read this if you are…

Technical people who are excellent at building things but struggle to get buy-in for their ideas
Anyone whose professional relationships could benefit from more intentional communication
Already well-read in communication — these ideas are foundational, not cutting-edge
Expecting psychological depth — this is practical, not academic
