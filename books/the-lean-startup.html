The Lean Startup

by Eric Ries — Read in 2023

Year Read: 2023
Recommended

Learning Summary

What this book is really about

The Lean Startup applies lean manufacturing principles to building companies and products. Eric Ries argues that most startups fail not because they can't build things, but because they build the wrong things. The solution: validate ideas with the minimum viable product (MVP) before investing heavily.

The core loop is Build → Measure → Learn: build a small version, measure how customers respond, and learn what to do next. Do this as fast as possible and you won't waste years on products nobody wants.

As someone building ML tools and products at Toolly, this book directly shaped how I think about launching AI features: ship a working prototype, measure real usage, then invest engineering time based on evidence — not assumptions.

"The only way to win is to learn faster than anyone else." — Eric Ries

Key Lessons

4 ideas that changed how I build

1
Validated learning beats vanity metrics
Page views and download numbers feel good but don't tell you if you're building something valuable. Validated learning means running experiments that produce real evidence. Ask: would a customer pay for this? Use it again? Refer a friend?
2
The MVP is the fastest path to learning, not a crappy product
The MVP is sometimes a landing page, a wizard-of-Oz demo, or one feature delivered manually. The goal is to answer: do people want this, and why? Build to learn, not to impress.
3
Pivot or persevere — don't just keep building blindly
A pivot (strategic course correction based on learning) is not failure. Perseverance is only valuable if the signal supports it. Many teams waste years by never stopping to ask: are we building the right thing?
4
Build an engine of growth, not one-off campaigns
Three growth engines: sticky (retention), viral (each user brings new users), and paid. Understanding which engine your product runs on determines which metrics actually matter — and where to invest next.

INTRODUCTION: A NEW WAY TO BUILD COMPANIES

Eric Ries opens The Lean Startup with a confession about failure. Before writing this book, he was part of a startup called IMVU that made nearly every classic startup mistake. The company spent months building a product in secret, convinced they knew exactly what customers wanted, only to launch and discover that almost everything they assumed was wrong.

But IMVU did not die from those mistakes. It survived and eventually thrived, and the reason it survived was that Ries and his co-founders developed a different way of thinking about startup success and failure. They stopped treating product development as an exercise in executing a plan and started treating it as an exercise in learning. They stopped measuring progress by features shipped and started measuring it by validated learning about customers.

This shift in thinking is the foundation of the Lean Startup methodology. It is not a collection of tactics for specific industries or specific types of companies. It is a way of thinking about the fundamental problem of entrepreneurship: how do you build a sustainable business when you cannot know in advance whether your assumptions about customers, markets, and products are correct?

Ries draws on his experience at IMVU and as an advisor to dozens of other startups to build a comprehensive framework for answering this question. He also draws on the principles of lean manufacturing, particularly the Toyota Production System, which revolutionized manufacturing in the twentieth century by focusing on eliminating waste and learning rapidly.

The Lean Startup applies these manufacturing principles to the chaotic, uncertain world of startups. The result is a methodology that is both rigorous and practical, grounded in real experience with real companies across many different industries.

The book is organized around three main ideas: the build-measure-learn feedback loop, the importance of validated learning as the true measure of startup progress, and the concept of the pivot as a structured course correction based on learning. Everything else in the book elaborates on these three ideas and provides practical guidance for implementing them.

Before diving into the chapters, it is important to understand what Ries means by startup. He uses the term very broadly. A startup is not just a small technology company in Silicon Valley. A startup is any human institution designed to create a new product or service under conditions of extreme uncertainty. By this definition, a new division within a large corporation is a startup. A nonprofit organization launching a new program is a startup. A government agency building a new service is a startup. The Lean Startup methodology applies to all of them.

Section

PART ONE: VISION

Chapter One: Start

1
Entrepreneurship Is Management

Ries begins with a fundamental reframing of what entrepreneurship is. Most people think of entrepreneurship as a personality type or a set of characteristics: risk-taking, visionary, independent. Ries argues that entrepreneurship is primarily a management discipline.

This is not a popular claim. The dominant cultural narrative about successful entrepreneurs celebrates the visionary who defies conventional wisdom, trusts their gut, and builds something the world did not know it needed. The story of Steve Jobs, Bill Gates, and other legendary entrepreneurs seems to confirm this narrative.

But Ries argues that this narrative is dangerously misleading for the vast majority of people who start companies. The legendary entrepreneurs who succeeded by trusting their vision are famous precisely because they are exceptional. For every visionary who built a billion-dollar company by trusting their instincts, there are thousands who failed the same way and whose stories are never told.

The problem with the prevailing approach to startups, Ries argues, is not that entrepreneurs are taking risks. Taking risks is inherent to entrepreneurship. The problem is that most startups take the wrong kinds of risks: they invest enormous resources in building products based on untested assumptions about what customers want, and they find out whether those assumptions are correct only when the product launches and customers respond.

This approach produces two failure modes. The first is building something that nobody wants, which is the most common startup failure. The second is succeeding slowly and expensively when a faster and cheaper path to the same success was available.

Entrepreneurship as a management discipline means applying rigor and systematic thinking to the process of testing assumptions and learning from customers. It means treating the startup as a laboratory for testing hypotheses about the business, not as a manufacturing plant for executing a predetermined plan.

2
Validated Learning - The True Measure of Startup Progress

Ries introduces what he considers the most important concept in the book: validated learning.

In traditional business thinking, progress is measured by outputs: revenue, customers, features shipped, meetings held. In startup thinking, these outputs are often misleading because they can be achieved without actually validating whether the business is on the right track.

You can ship features without validating that those features serve a genuine customer need. You can acquire customers without validating that those customers are the right customers. You can generate revenue without validating that the revenue model is sustainable.

Validated learning is different. It is learning about what is actually true about your business, derived from real experiments with real customers. When you run an experiment, collect data, and update your understanding of what your customers want and what will make your business work, that is validated learning.

Ries argues that validated learning should replace traditional measures of progress. The question should not be: how many features did we ship this month? The question should be: what did we learn this month about our customers and our business, and how confident are we in that learning?

This reframing changes everything about how a startup operates. Instead of building elaborate products based on assumptions, you build small, quick experiments to test those assumptions. Instead of measuring success by what you built, you measure it by what you learned.

Validated learning is more valuable than features, revenue, or customers in the early stages of a startup because it is the foundation on which sustainable growth is built. A startup that has validated that customers genuinely want their product and that they have found a sustainable business model is in a far stronger position than a startup that has shipped many features but has not validated any of its core assumptions.

3
The IMVU Story - Learning From Failure the Right Way

Ries tells the story of IMVU in detail to illustrate the difference between traditional and lean approaches to startup development.

IMVU's original product was an instant messaging add-on that would allow people to communicate using animated 3D avatars. Ries and his co-founders spent months building this product based on their assumptions about what customers wanted. They assumed that users would want to use their existing social networks and import their existing contacts into the new platform.

When they launched, they discovered that almost every assumption was wrong. Users did not want to import their existing contacts because the social dynamics of using animated avatars were different from regular messaging. They were embarrassed to invite existing friends and would only use the product with strangers they met through IMVU itself.

This was a crucial discovery, but IMVU would have discovered it much faster if they had started with smaller experiments rather than spending months building a full product. They had spent enormous engineering resources building contact import features that no one used.

After this experience, Ries and his co-founders adopted what he calls the build-measure-learn approach. Instead of building complete features, they would build the minimum version of a feature necessary to learn whether customers wanted it. Instead of launching every six months with a big release, they would release new versions multiple times per week and measure how customers responded.

This approach accelerated their learning dramatically. They stopped spending months building things nobody wanted and started learning what customers actually needed within days or weeks. The company's growth rate improved significantly after they adopted this approach.

Chapter Two: Define

4
Who Is an Entrepreneur?

Ries expands the definition of entrepreneur to include anyone who is trying to create something new under conditions of uncertainty. This is a deliberate attempt to make the Lean Startup methodology relevant to people who would not normally think of themselves as entrepreneurs.

The product manager at a large corporation who is trying to launch a new internal tool is an entrepreneur. The employee who proposes a new way of serving customers is an entrepreneur. The government official who is trying to improve a public service is an entrepreneur.

This broad definition matters because the conditions of uncertainty that characterize startup thinking are not limited to small technology companies. Whenever you are trying to create something new without knowing in advance whether it will work, the Lean Startup methodology is relevant.

Ries is also addressing a specific failure mode that afflicts large organizations: the tendency to apply traditional management techniques to entrepreneurial challenges. Traditional management is very good at executing a known plan efficiently. It is very bad at finding the right plan when you do not know in advance what the plan should be. The Lean Startup methodology provides the tools for finding the right plan.

5
The Definition of a Startup

Ries provides his formal definition of a startup: a human institution designed to create a new product or service under conditions of extreme uncertainty.

Several words in this definition are important.

Human institution: A startup is first and foremost a collection of people with a shared mission. It is not primarily a technology or a product. The technology and product are tools the people use to pursue the mission.

Designed to create: A startup is intentional. It is not a random assembly of people or activities. It is an organized effort to build something specific.

New product or service: The startup is doing something that has not been done in this particular way before. This is what creates the extreme uncertainty.

Extreme uncertainty: This is the defining characteristic of startups and the reason why traditional management approaches are insufficient. When you are doing something truly new, you cannot know in advance whether it will work. The only way to find out is to try.

Chapter Three: Learn

6
Rethinking How We Measure Progress

Traditional metrics for measuring startup progress are what Ries calls vanity metrics: numbers that look good but do not actually tell you whether your business is working.

The classic vanity metric is total registered users. A startup can report millions of registered users and still be in deep trouble if those users are not engaged, not paying, and not growing through genuine word-of-mouth. The impressive number hides the underlying reality.

Other common vanity metrics include total website visits, total app downloads, total emails sent, and total features shipped. These numbers can all grow impressively while the business is fundamentally broken.

Ries contrasts vanity metrics with what he calls actionable metrics: numbers that demonstrate a clear causal relationship between something the team did and an outcome that matters.

An actionable metric shows you that when you made a specific change, a specific outcome improved. This gives you information you can act on. A vanity metric just shows you that a number went up or down, without telling you why or what to do about it.

The discipline of using actionable metrics requires much more rigor than tracking vanity metrics. You need to design experiments, isolate variables, and measure outcomes carefully. But the payoff is genuine learning: knowledge of what actually works in your business.

7
Actionable vs. Vanity Metrics

Ries elaborates on the distinction between actionable and vanity metrics with a specific example.

Imagine a startup that tracks total registered users. Every month, the number goes up. The team celebrates. But the team has learned nothing from this number because they do not know why it went up. Was it because of a new marketing campaign? A feature change? Seasonal variation? Pure luck?

Without knowing why the number went up, the team cannot replicate the success. Without knowing whether the registered users are actually engaged with the product, the team does not know whether the business is working.

Now imagine the same startup tracks the cohort retention rate: what percentage of users who registered in a given week are still active three months later? This metric is actionable because it tells you whether the product is providing enough value to keep users coming back.

If the retention rate is high, the team knows the product is working for the users who find it. They can focus on acquisition. If the retention rate is low, the team knows there is a fundamental problem with the product that acquisition cannot fix. Getting more users to leave quickly is not a path to success.

The actionable metric tells the team what to work on next. The vanity metric just makes them feel good.

8
Learning Milestones as an Alternative to Traditional Milestones

Traditional startup planning uses product development milestones: when will version one launch? When will we have ten thousand users? When will we close our first enterprise deal?

Ries proposes replacing these product milestones with learning milestones: specific validated learnings that the team plans to achieve by specific dates.

A learning milestone might be: by the end of this month, we will know whether our target customers experience the problem we think they have in the way we think they have it. Or: by the end of this quarter, we will know whether our proposed solution reduces the cost of that problem enough to justify the price we want to charge.

Learning milestones make the work of learning rigorous and accountable. The team commits to specific experiments, runs them, and evaluates whether the planned learning was achieved. This accountability prevents the common startup failure mode of spending months building without learning anything.

PART TWO: STEER

Chapter Four: Experiment

9
The Scientific Method Applied to Business

The experimental approach to business is the heart of the Lean Startup methodology. Ries argues that every business assumption should be treated as a hypothesis to be tested rather than a fact to be assumed.

When you are starting a new business, you have many assumptions. You assume customers have a particular problem. You assume they will pay a particular price to solve it. You assume your solution will solve it better than alternatives. You assume your marketing message will reach them. You assume your business model will be profitable at scale.

Traditional startup thinking treats these assumptions as given and builds the business on top of them. Lean startup thinking treats them as hypotheses and designs experiments to test them before building on top of them.

The distinction matters enormously. When you treat assumptions as facts, you invest heavily in executing on those assumptions before discovering whether they are true. When a key assumption turns out to be wrong, the investment is lost. When you treat assumptions as hypotheses, you invest in testing them before committing to them. Failed hypotheses are cheap failures; you learn quickly that the assumption was wrong and move on.

10
The Two Most Important Assumptions - Value and Growth Hypotheses

Ries identifies two categories of assumptions that are most critical to test in any new business.

The value hypothesis asks whether the product or service actually delivers value to the customers who use it. This is different from asking whether customers will buy the product. A customer might buy a product and then discover it does not actually solve their problem. The value hypothesis is validated when you have evidence that customers are genuinely benefiting from the product in a measurable way.

The growth hypothesis asks how new customers will discover the product. Every successful business grows through some mechanism: word of mouth, paid advertising, viral loops, sales teams, partnerships. The growth hypothesis articulates what that mechanism will be and can be tested by examining early growth data.

Both hypotheses must be validated for a business to succeed. A product that delivers genuine value but that customers do not discover will not grow. A product that grows quickly through some mechanism but that does not deliver genuine value will lose customers as quickly as it acquires them.

Testing these two hypotheses rigorously, before investing heavily in scaling the business, is the most important work a startup can do.

11
The Story of Zappos - A Classic Lean Experiment

Ries uses the founding story of Zappos as a canonical example of a lean experiment that validated critical assumptions before major investment.

Nick Swinmurn, the founder of Zappos, wanted to test the hypothesis that people would buy shoes online. Before building an elaborate e-commerce platform, before negotiating with shoe manufacturers, before investing in inventory, he ran a simple experiment.

He went to local shoe stores, took photos of their inventory, and listed the shoes on a simple website. When someone ordered a shoe through his website, he would go back to the store, buy the shoe at retail price, and ship it to the customer.

This was not a profitable business model. He was buying shoes at retail and selling them at retail, plus incurring the costs of his own time and shipping. But profitability was not the goal of this experiment. The goal was to test whether people would actually buy shoes online.

The experiment validated the hypothesis. People bought shoes through his website. Swinmurn had the evidence he needed to justify building a real business, with actual inventory, real vendor relationships, and a proper e-commerce platform. Zappos eventually became the largest online shoe retailer in the world and was acquired by Amazon for over a billion dollars.

The lesson Ries draws from this story is that the most important question for a new business is almost never how to build the product. The most important question is whether the product is worth building at all. And the best way to answer that question is to run a cheap, quick experiment that tests the core assumption, not to build the full product and hope the assumption is correct.

12
The Minimum Viable Product

This is the concept that has become most closely associated with the Lean Startup methodology, and Ries's treatment of it is worth understanding carefully because it is frequently misunderstood.

A minimum viable product, or MVP, is the version of a new product that allows a team to collect the maximum amount of validated learning about customers with the least effort.

Notice what this definition does not say. It does not say the MVP is the simplest possible version of the product. It does not say the MVP is a buggy, incomplete product. It does not say the MVP is what customers will eventually use. It says the MVP is the version that collects the most learning with the least effort.

This definition means that the right MVP depends entirely on what you are trying to learn. If you are trying to learn whether customers have a particular problem, your MVP might be a survey or a series of customer interviews. If you are trying to learn whether customers will pay for a solution, your MVP might be a landing page with a buy button that collects email addresses or payment information before the product is built. If you are trying to learn how customers use a solution, your MVP might be a manually operated version of the service before any automation is built.

The MVP is a learning tool, not a product release. It is designed to answer specific questions about the business, not to satisfy all customer needs.

13
The Concierge MVP

One specific type of MVP that Ries discusses is what he calls the concierge MVP. This is when the service that will eventually be automated is delivered manually by the founders in order to learn what customers actually need before building any technology.

The name comes from a company called Food on the Table, which built a service that helped families plan meals and create shopping lists based on local grocery sales. Before building any software, the founders personally went to customers' homes, found out what they liked to eat, drove to local grocery stores to find what was on sale, and came back with personalized meal plans and shopping lists.

This was obviously not scalable. One person can serve only a handful of customers this way. But it was extraordinarily educational. The founders learned directly what customers valued, what frustrated them, and what would make the service genuinely useful. They used this learning to build software that actually worked for customers rather than software based on their assumptions about what customers needed.

The concierge MVP works in any service business where the core activity can be performed manually before being automated. It is particularly valuable because it forces founders to do the work themselves rather than imagining how customers will interact with an automated system.

14
Quality and the MVP - A Common Misunderstanding

Ries addresses a common objection to the MVP concept: that shipping a low-quality product will damage the brand and drive away early customers.

He acknowledges this concern but argues it is usually based on a misunderstanding of what the MVP is supposed to do. The purpose of the MVP is not to launch a product to the mass market. It is to test specific hypotheses with a small group of early adopters.

Early adopters are a specific kind of customer who are particularly forgiving of product imperfection because they are excited about new solutions to problems they care deeply about. They are not the customers who will be served once the product is mature. They are the customers who help you learn what the mature product should be.

Ries also makes a pragmatic argument: the cost of building a perfect product that nobody wants is much higher than the cost of shipping an imperfect product to a small group of early adopters to learn whether the core concept works. The risk of brand damage from an imperfect MVP is far smaller than the risk of building something nobody needs.

That said, Ries does not advocate for shipping genuinely broken or harmful products. The MVP should be the minimum version that can actually test the hypothesis you are trying to test. It should work well enough to allow customers to experience the core value proposition, even if it lacks all the features that the final product will have.

Chapter Five: Leap

15
Leap of Faith Assumptions

Ries introduces the concept of leap of faith assumptions: the assumptions that form the foundation of a new business and that must be true for the business to succeed.

Every new business rests on a set of assumptions that cannot be proven in advance. The founders must take a leap of faith and believe these assumptions are true before investing in the business. The question is not whether to make leap of faith assumptions. Every startup must make them. The question is how quickly and cheaply you can validate or invalidate those assumptions.

Ries identifies two types of leap of faith assumptions that are particularly important.

The value creation assumption: This is the assumption that the product or service creates real value for customers. Not the assumption that people will buy it, but the assumption that they will genuinely benefit from it in a way that changes their behavior.

The growth assumption: This is the assumption that there is a mechanism by which new customers will discover the product and the business can grow sustainably.

Both assumptions need to be tested early. Building a business on a value assumption that turns out to be wrong means building something customers do not actually benefit from. Building a business on a growth assumption that turns out to be wrong means building something that will never reach enough customers to become sustainable.

16
Genchi Gembutsu - Getting Out of the Building

Ries borrows a concept from Toyota called genchi gembutsu, which roughly translates to "go and see for yourself." In the Toyota Production System, this principle means that managers and engineers should observe actual production processes rather than relying on reports and abstractions.

Applied to startups, genchi gembutsu means getting out of the building to observe actual customers in their natural environment rather than making assumptions about them from the office.

Ries contrasts two approaches to customer research. The first is what he calls customer discovery through conversation, which means having structured conversations with potential customers to understand their problems, priorities, and current solutions. The second is observation, which means watching customers actually do the thing your product is supposed to help them with, in the environment where they normally do it.

Both approaches produce better learning than making assumptions without customer contact. But observation often reveals things that conversation does not, because what people say they do and what they actually do are often quite different.

The general principle is that all startup assumptions should be tested against reality, and reality means actual customers in actual situations, not imagined customers in idealized situations.

Chapter Six: Test

17
Building an MVP - What to Include and Exclude

Ries provides guidance on how to decide what to include in an MVP and what to leave out. The decision should always be driven by the question: what is the minimum we need to build to test our most important hypothesis?

This sounds simple but is actually quite difficult in practice. There is always pressure to add more features to the MVP: to make it more polished, more complete, more impressive. This pressure comes from several sources: engineers who are proud of their work and want to build something technically impressive, founders who are afraid that an incomplete product will not impress investors or customers, and the natural human desire to do things thoroughly.

Ries argues that these pressures should be resisted because they all lead to the same failure mode: spending too much time and money building before you know whether you are building the right thing.

The discipline of MVP thinking is the discipline of asking, for every proposed feature: what hypothesis does this feature test? If the feature is not testing a hypothesis, it should not be in the MVP. If the feature is testing a hypothesis, but a cheaper experiment could test the same hypothesis, that cheaper experiment should be used instead.

18
Removing Features From the MVP

Ries tells a story from his own experience at IMVU that illustrates the counterintuitive nature of MVP thinking.

IMVU wanted to add a feature that allowed users to walk their avatars through a 3D world, moving from room to room. This seemed like an obvious feature for a virtual world product. It would make the product more immersive and more exciting.

But Ries and his team calculated that adding this feature would take months of engineering work. Before committing to that investment, they asked: what hypothesis does this feature test? The hypothesis was that users would find a navigable 3D world more engaging than a single 3D room.

Rather than spending months building the full navigation feature, they ran a quick experiment. They simulated the navigation in a crude way that gave users the experience of moving between rooms without actually building the full system. Then they measured whether users who experienced this simulated navigation were more engaged than users who did not.

The result surprised them. Users did not become more engaged. The navigation feature, which seemed obviously valuable, did not actually change user behavior in a meaningful way. They had avoided months of wasted engineering effort by testing the hypothesis cheaply before building.

This story illustrates one of the most important lessons in the book: your intuitions about what customers want are often wrong, and the only way to know what customers actually want is to test those intuitions.

19
The A/B Test as an MVP Technique

Ries discusses the A/B test as one of the most powerful tools for validating startup hypotheses. An A/B test, also called a split test, is when you show two different versions of something to two different groups of users and measure which version produces better outcomes.

A/B tests are particularly valuable because they allow you to isolate the effect of a single change. If you change ten things at once and user engagement improves, you do not know which change caused the improvement. If you change one thing at a time and measure the effect, you build a genuine understanding of what drives outcomes in your business.

Ries gives the example of Obama's 2008 presidential campaign, which used extensive A/B testing on its website to optimize donations. The campaign tested different images, different button colors, different headline text, and different email subject lines. Through thousands of small experiments, they learned what actually motivated supporters to donate and improved their fundraising significantly.

The lesson is that A/B testing is not just for technology companies. Any organization that can measure outcomes and run controlled experiments can use A/B testing to learn what drives those outcomes.

Chapter Seven: Measure

20
Innovation Accounting - A New Way to Measure Progress

This chapter introduces one of Ries's most important and most practical concepts: innovation accounting.

Traditional accounting measures revenue, costs, and profit. These measures are appropriate for established businesses running known business models. For startups that are trying to discover a business model, traditional accounting measures are misleading because they measure outputs of a business that does not yet have a validated foundation.

Innovation accounting is a framework for measuring startup progress in terms of validated learning. Instead of asking how much revenue we made this month, it asks: how much did we learn this month about whether our business will work?

Innovation accounting operates in three steps.

Step one: Use an MVP to establish a baseline. Run your first experiments to understand where you actually are today. Measure your conversion rates, retention rates, and other key metrics at their current baseline state. This gives you a starting point against which to measure future improvement.

Step two: Tune the engine from the baseline toward the ideal. Run experiments to improve the key metrics. Each experiment tests a specific hypothesis: if we change this thing, this metric will improve. Measure the results rigorously. Learn what actually moves the metrics and what does not.

Step three: Decide whether to pivot or persevere. Based on what you have learned about what does and does not move the key metrics, decide whether your current strategy can get you to your goals or whether you need to fundamentally change your approach.

This three-step process makes startup progress measurable and accountable in a way that traditional product development does not.

21
The Three A's of Metrics

Ries specifies three properties that good startup metrics must have. He calls these the three A's.

Actionable: The metric must demonstrate clear cause and effect. When the metric changes, you know why it changed and what you can do to move it further. If you cannot identify a clear action that caused a change in the metric, the metric is not actionable.

Accessible: The metric must be understandable to the people who need to act on it. If the metric requires complex statistical analysis to understand, most people in the organization will not engage with it. Simple, concrete metrics that can be understood intuitively are more actionable than complex ones.

Auditable: The metric must be trustworthy. The people using the metric must be able to verify it by looking at individual data points and making sure the numbers make sense. A metric that people suspect is misleading will not be acted upon.

These three properties together define the kind of metrics that drive genuine learning and improvement.

22
Cohort Analysis - A Better Way to Measure User Behavior

Ries introduces cohort analysis as one of the most powerful tools for measuring startup progress. Cohort analysis is a technique for grouping users by when they first interacted with your product and measuring their behavior separately for each group.

Without cohort analysis, it is easy to be fooled by aggregate metrics. If you have a thousand users and your average session length is three minutes, you do not know whether your product is getting better or worse over time. Maybe the users who signed up recently are spending five minutes per session, but they are diluted in the average by older users who have disengaged and only spend one minute.

With cohort analysis, you look at each group separately. You look at users who signed up in January and measure their session length over time. You look at users who signed up in February and measure their session length over time. By comparing these cohorts, you can see clearly whether newer users are behaving better or worse than older ones.

If each successive cohort shows improving engagement, you know your product improvements are working. If each successive cohort shows declining engagement, you know something is getting worse despite your efforts. This kind of insight is impossible with aggregate metrics.

Cohort analysis is particularly important for understanding the effect of product changes. When you make a change to your product, you want to know whether users who signed up after the change are having a better experience than users who signed up before the change. Cohort analysis makes this comparison possible.

23
Kanban and the Validation of Product Development

Ries discusses how to connect the product development process to validated learning using a technique adapted from lean manufacturing.

He describes a product development system used by a startup where work items were limited to three states: product backlog, in progress, and built. But there was an additional constraint: before a feature could be moved from built to the next stage, it had to be validated by actual user data.

This system prevented the common startup failure mode of building many features and then discovering that most of them do not actually improve the business. By requiring validation before moving on, the system forced the team to confront the reality of whether each feature was actually working.

The practical lesson is that product development and learning must be tightly coupled. Building features that are never validated is waste in the lean manufacturing sense: effort that does not contribute to the ultimate goal. The validation requirement makes this waste visible and forces teams to confront it.

Chapter Eight: Pivot (or Persevere)

24
The Pivot - One of the Most Important Concepts in Startup Thinking

The pivot is one of the most widely used and most widely misunderstood concepts in startup culture. Ries defines it precisely: a pivot is a structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis about the product, strategy, and engine of growth.

Several words in this definition are important.

Structured: A pivot is not a random change made out of desperation. It is a deliberate decision made based on specific evidence that the current strategy is not working.

Course correction: A pivot changes something fundamental about the strategy, not just a minor feature or implementation detail.

New fundamental hypothesis: A pivot tests a different assumption about what will make the business work.

The decision to pivot or persevere is one of the hardest and most important decisions a startup makes. Pivoting too early means giving up on a strategy before giving it a fair chance. Pivoting too late means continuing to invest in a strategy that the evidence clearly shows is not working.

Ries argues that most startups pivot too late. The reason is psychological: founders and teams become emotionally invested in their original vision, and changing it feels like admitting failure. But failing to pivot when the evidence demands it is a far more costly mistake than pivoting too early.

25
Signs That You Need to Pivot

Ries provides specific indicators that a pivot may be necessary.

The decreasing effectiveness of product experiments. When you run experiments and the results keep coming back flat, when no experiment seems to move the key metrics significantly, this is a sign that your fundamental assumptions may be wrong. You can optimize an incorrect strategy for a long time without ever getting to a good outcome.

The team's morale and motivation declining. When the team stops believing that the current strategy can succeed, they start going through the motions without genuine engagement. The innovative energy that drives successful startups dissipates. This is a leading indicator of failure.

The startup's runway decreasing without validated learning increasing proportionally. If you are spending money without generating corresponding validated learning, you are burning resources that will eventually run out.

Ries recommends holding regular pivot-or-persevere meetings where the team honestly evaluates the evidence and asks whether the current strategy is working or whether a pivot is needed.

26
Types of Pivots

Ries catalogs the different kinds of pivots that startups can make. Understanding the different types helps founders think more precisely about what specifically needs to change.

The zoom-in pivot: What was previously considered a feature of the product becomes the entire product. A startup originally building a broad social platform might discover that one specific feature is extraordinarily valuable and pivot to making that feature the entire focus.

The zoom-out pivot: The opposite of the zoom-in pivot. What was previously the entire product becomes a feature of a larger product. A startup originally building a single tool discovers that customers need a broader platform and pivots to build it.

The customer segment pivot: The product works, but for a different customer than originally targeted. The startup pivots to serve the customers who are actually getting value from the product rather than the customers they originally intended to serve.

The customer need pivot: The problem the startup was solving turns out not to be important enough to customers, but in the course of understanding customers, the startup discovers a different, more important problem they can solve with their existing capabilities.

The platform pivot: A startup originally building an application pivots to building a platform on which other applications can be built. Or vice versa.

The business architecture pivot: A startup originally pursuing a high-margin, low-volume business model pivots to a low-margin, high-volume model. Or vice versa.

The value capture pivot: The startup changes how it monetizes the value it creates. A startup might move from a subscription model to a transaction fee model, or from a consumer-paid model to an advertiser-supported model.

The engine of growth pivot: The startup changes the mechanism by which it grows. A startup originally growing through viral mechanisms might pivot to paid acquisition or vice versa.

The channel pivot: The startup changes the mechanism by which it delivers its product or service to customers. A startup selling through a direct sales force might pivot to selling through resellers or through a self-serve online channel.

The technology pivot: The startup discovers a better way to achieve the same solution using a different technology. The problem being solved and the customer being served remain the same, but the technical approach changes.

27
The Pivot Requires Courage

Ries emphasizes that pivoting is not just a strategic or analytical challenge. It is a psychological and organizational challenge.

Founders are often deeply committed to their original vision. They have invested significant time, energy, and emotion in it. They have recruited team members who believe in it. They have made promises to investors about it. Changing direction feels like admitting they were wrong, which feels like failure.

But Ries argues that this psychological resistance is one of the primary reasons startups fail. The entrepreneur who cannot pivot when the evidence demands it will continue executing a failing strategy until they run out of time and money.

The key reframe is to think of a pivot not as an admission of failure but as an application of learning. The original strategy was a hypothesis. The experiments revealed that the hypothesis was wrong. Pivoting is the rational response to learning. It is not failure; it is scientific progress.

Ries also argues that startups that pivot based on evidence are not just responding to failure. They are often discovering something genuinely valuable: a real problem that customers genuinely have, a real mechanism for solving it, a real business model for sustaining the solution.

PART THREE: ACCELERATE

Chapter Nine: Batch

28
The Power of Small Batches

Ries introduces one of the most important principles from lean manufacturing and explains why it is equally applicable to software development and startup processes: small batches are more efficient than large batches.

This is counterintuitive. It seems like it would be more efficient to do many things at once than to do them one at a time. But lean manufacturing discovered that this intuition is wrong, and software development and startup thinking have confirmed the same finding.

Ries uses a simple example to illustrate. Suppose you need to fold a hundred letters, stuff them in envelopes, seal the envelopes, and stamp them. The batch approach would have you fold all hundred letters, then stuff them all, then seal them all, then stamp them all.

The single-piece flow approach would have you fold a letter, stuff it, seal it, and stamp it, then do the same for the next letter.

The batch approach seems faster because each motion is repeated without interruption. But the single-piece flow approach is actually faster in most situations because it reveals problems immediately. If the letter does not fit in the envelope, you discover this with the first letter and fix it before completing ninety-nine more letters that also do not fit. The batch approach would not reveal this problem until you try to stuff all hundred letters.

In startup terms, small batches mean releasing changes and testing hypotheses frequently rather than waiting for large releases. Small batches reveal problems quickly, when they are cheap to fix. Large batches hide problems until they accumulate into large, expensive failures.

29
Continuous Deployment and the Lean Startup

Ries discusses the concept of continuous deployment as an application of the small batch principle to software development.

Continuous deployment is the practice of releasing new code to production as soon as it is ready, rather than accumulating many changes into large periodic releases. Companies like Amazon and Flickr deploy new code dozens or hundreds of times per day.

The lean startup benefits of continuous deployment are several. First, bugs are caught quickly because each deployment changes only a small number of things, making it easy to identify what caused any new problem. Second, learning is faster because you can test new ideas with real users within hours rather than weeks or months. Third, the integration problems that arise when large amounts of code are combined are avoided because integrations happen continuously in small increments.

The cultural shift required for continuous deployment is significant. It requires robust automated testing, a willingness to trust the process of frequent small releases, and a shift away from the traditional waterfall model of software development in which large batches of work accumulate before release.

30
Pull vs. Push and the Lean Startup

Ries explains the lean manufacturing concept of pull versus push and applies it to startup product development.

In a push system, you produce based on forecasts. You predict what customers will want, produce it in advance, and push it to customers. This approach requires accurate forecasting to avoid over-production and waste.

In a pull system, you produce in response to actual demand. Customers signal what they want, and you produce it in response to that signal. This approach eliminates the waste of over-production but requires a highly responsive production system.

In startup terms, the push approach is to build a product based on assumptions about what customers will want and then release it hoping they will adopt it. The pull approach is to test hypotheses about what customers want, confirm that they actually want it, and then build only what has been validated.

The Lean Startup methodology is fundamentally a pull approach. Everything is built in response to validated learning about what customers actually need rather than in anticipation of what they might need.

Chapter Ten: Grow

31
The Three Engines of Growth

One of the most important contributions of The Lean Startup is Ries's framework for understanding how startups grow. He identifies three distinct engines of growth and argues that understanding which engine drives your business is essential for making good decisions about where to invest.

The Sticky Engine of Growth

The sticky engine relies on high customer retention. If customers who sign up for your product continue using it over time, and if you acquire new customers faster than you lose old ones, the business grows.

The key metric for the sticky engine is churn rate: the percentage of customers who stop using the product in any given period. If the churn rate is higher than the new customer acquisition rate, the business shrinks. If new customer acquisition exceeds churn, the business grows.

For businesses using the sticky engine, the most important work is improving the product to reduce churn. Acquiring more customers is of limited value if you are losing existing customers at a high rate. Ries uses the analogy of a leaky bucket: filling a leaky bucket faster does not help as much as fixing the leaks.

The Viral Engine of Growth

The viral engine relies on customers bringing in other customers as a natural result of using the product. When each customer, on average, recruits more than one additional customer, the business grows exponentially without any additional marketing spend.

The key metric for the viral engine is the viral coefficient: how many new customers each existing customer generates on average. If the viral coefficient is greater than one, the business grows exponentially. If it is less than one, viral growth alone cannot sustain the business.

For businesses using the viral engine, the most important work is making the product something that customers naturally share with others. This might mean making sharing a core part of the product experience, or making the product more valuable when more of your contacts use it.

The Paid Engine of Growth

The paid engine relies on acquiring customers through paid marketing channels. The key insight for this engine is the relationship between the lifetime value of a customer and the cost of acquiring them.

If you can acquire a customer for five dollars and that customer generates twenty-five dollars of revenue over their lifetime, you have a viable paid acquisition model. You can invest more in customer acquisition and grow the business as long as this ratio holds.

The key metrics for the paid engine are customer lifetime value and customer acquisition cost. If the lifetime value exceeds the acquisition cost, you can pour money into acquisition and grow. If the acquisition cost approaches or exceeds the lifetime value, growth through paid acquisition is not sustainable.

32
Which Engine Should You Use?

Ries argues that most successful businesses are primarily powered by one engine of growth, although multiple engines can operate simultaneously. Trying to optimize all three engines at once is usually a mistake because the optimizations for each are often different and even contradictory.

The right engine depends on the nature of the business. A social network naturally uses the viral engine because social networks are more valuable when more of your friends use them. A SaaS business that requires ongoing subscription payments naturally uses the sticky engine because the revenue depends on retaining customers. A business selling products to large enterprises naturally uses the paid engine because the high value of each customer justifies significant acquisition costs.

Understanding which engine drives your business tells you which metrics matter most and where to focus optimization efforts. A social network that focuses on paid acquisition is optimizing the wrong engine. A SaaS business that focuses on viral growth while ignoring churn is also optimizing the wrong engine.

33
Optimizing the Engine vs. Changing the Engine

Ries makes an important distinction between two types of growth strategy: optimizing the current engine of growth and changing to a different engine.

Optimizing the current engine means making incremental improvements to the mechanisms that are already driving growth. If you are using the viral engine, optimizing it means increasing the viral coefficient: making the product more shareable, more valuable when shared, more likely to generate referrals.

Changing the engine is a more fundamental shift. If the sticky engine is not working because your product does not retain users, and if no amount of product improvement seems to fix the retention problem, you might need to consider whether the viral or paid engine would be more appropriate for your business.

Changing engines is essentially a form of pivot. It requires the same kind of evidence-based decision-making and the same kind of courage to change fundamental strategy based on what the data shows.

Chapter Eleven: Adapt

34
Adaptive Organizations - Building the Ability to Learn

Ries argues that the most important organizational capability for a startup is adaptability: the ability to change course quickly in response to new learning. This requires building organizations and processes that support learning and adaptation rather than organizations and processes optimized purely for execution efficiency.

Traditional organizations are optimized for execution. They have clear hierarchies, specialized roles, standardized processes, and performance metrics focused on execution of known tasks. This makes them very efficient at doing things they already know how to do but very poor at discovering new things to do.

Adaptive organizations maintain execution capability but also invest in the organizational structures that support learning. This means creating time and space for experimentation, rewarding employees for validated learning rather than just for execution, and making it safe to question assumptions and report negative results.

35
The Five Whys - A Tool for Root Cause Analysis

Ries devotes significant attention to a specific technique from Toyota that he considers essential for adaptive organizations: the Five Whys.

The Five Whys is a root cause analysis technique developed by Taiichi Ohno, the creator of the Toyota Production System. The technique is simple: when a problem occurs, ask "why" five times, each time asking why the answer to the previous question is true. By the time you have asked why five times, you have usually reached the root cause of the problem rather than its surface symptom.

Ries gives an example from a software startup. A website goes down. Why did the website go down? Because the server ran out of memory. Why did the server run out of memory? Because a new code deployment included a memory leak. Why was the code deployed with a memory leak? Because the engineer did not test memory usage before deploying. Why did the engineer not test memory usage? Because there is no standard testing procedure for memory usage. Why is there no standard testing procedure? Because the team has never prioritized it.

The Five Whys reveals that the surface problem, the server running out of memory, has a root cause, the absence of memory testing procedures, that is very different from the surface symptom. Fixing the surface problem, adding more memory to the server, would not prevent the same kind of problem in the future. Fixing the root cause, establishing memory testing procedures, prevents the entire class of problems.

36
Applying the Five Whys to Startup Problems

Ries shows how the Five Whys applies to startup problems beyond technical failures.

When a startup problem occurs, whether it is low customer retention, poor conversion rates, slow sales cycles, or any other business problem, the Five Whys technique can reveal root causes that are not obvious from the surface symptom.

A startup with poor customer retention might discover, through Five Whys analysis, that the root cause is inadequate onboarding, which causes new users to not understand how to use the product effectively, which causes them to not experience the core value, which causes them to not return.

A startup with slow sales cycles might discover, through Five Whys analysis, that the root cause is that salespeople are targeting the wrong kind of customer, who does not have a strong enough need for the product to justify the purchase decision quickly.

Ries notes that the Five Whys often reveals organizational problems at its root. Technical problems, customer problems, and business problems frequently trace back to organizational issues: lack of training, unclear ownership, inadequate processes, or poor communication. This is why the Five Whys is valuable beyond technical troubleshooting.

37
The Five Whys Requires Blamelessness

Ries emphasizes an important cultural requirement for the Five Whys to work: the analysis must be blameless.

If the Five Whys process is used to identify whose fault a problem is, people will become defensive and dishonest. The analysis will stop at the first person who can be blamed rather than reaching the root organizational cause.

If the Five Whys process is genuinely blameless, focused on what processes and systems failed rather than who failed, people can be honest about what happened and the analysis can reach genuine root causes.

This blamelessness is harder to achieve than it sounds. Organizations have strong cultural pressures toward assigning blame. Managers who have built their careers on execution excellence are often reluctant to acknowledge that their processes or structures are the root cause of problems. Creating a genuinely blameless culture requires deliberate effort and leadership commitment.

Chapter Twelve: Innovate

38
Building a Platform for Experimentation Within Established Companies

As companies grow and become successful, they face a new challenge: maintaining the ability to innovate while also executing reliably on what already works. Ries calls this the innovator's dilemma, referencing Clayton Christensen's famous work on how established companies fail to respond to disruptive innovation.

The Lean Startup methodology provides tools for addressing this challenge. Established companies can create internal structures, sometimes called innovation labs or internal incubators, that apply lean startup principles to new ventures while the main organization continues to execute on the established business.

Ries provides practical guidance on how these internal innovation structures should work.

They need to have scarce resources but not too scarce. Internal startups need enough resources to actually run experiments, but scarcity forces them to prioritize ruthlessly.

They must be independent of the main organization's performance review process. If internal startup teams are evaluated using the same metrics as the main organization, they will optimize for those metrics rather than for learning and innovation.

They need protected but not isolated physical space. Teams working on innovation need to be able to work together without constant interruption, but they also need access to the main organization's customers, knowledge, and capabilities.

39
The Innovation Sandbox

Ries introduces the concept of the innovation sandbox: a designated space within a larger organization where teams can run experiments with real customers using real systems, but in a controlled way that limits the potential for harm to the existing business.

The innovation sandbox defines the boundaries of what a team can change or test without going through the full organizational approval process. Within the sandbox, the team can move quickly and experiment freely. Outside the sandbox, they must work through normal organizational channels.

The sandbox makes it possible for large organizations to run lean startup experiments at meaningful scale without creating organizational chaos. The boundaries prevent experiments from inadvertently affecting parts of the business that are not part of the experiment.

As an experiment generates validated learning and builds a track record, the sandbox can be expanded. Successful experiments can eventually graduate from the sandbox to become part of the main organization.

40
The Three Structural Attributes of Internal Innovation Teams

Ries identifies three structural attributes that internal innovation teams need to be effective.

Scarce but secure resources: The team needs enough resources to run meaningful experiments, but the resources should not be so abundant that scarcity fails to drive prioritization. The resources also need to be secure: the team should not be in constant danger of having their resources redirected to the main business.

Independent authority to develop their business: The team needs the authority to make decisions about product direction, customer targeting, and business model without requiring approval from the main organization for every decision.

Personal stake in the outcome: Team members need to be genuinely invested in the success of the venture. This might mean equity-like incentives, recognition for successful ventures, or other mechanisms that align personal incentives with team success.

THE BUILD-MEASURE-LEARN FEEDBACK LOOP IN DETAIL

Lesson 41: The Build-Measure-Learn Loop Is the Core Engine

The build-measure-learn feedback loop is the central organizing framework of the Lean Startup methodology. Everything else in the book is in service of running this loop as quickly and efficiently as possible.

The loop has three stages.

Build: Create the minimum viable product necessary to test your most important hypothesis.

Measure: Collect the data generated by customer interaction with the MVP. Use rigorous metrics that are actionable, accessible, and auditable.

Learn: Analyze the data to validate or invalidate your hypotheses. Use this learning to decide whether to pivot or persevere.

Then start the loop again, incorporating what you learned into the next build.

The goal is to minimize the time required to complete each iteration of the loop. The faster you complete the loop, the more iterations you can run, the more hypotheses you can test, and the faster you learn what will make your business work.

Ries argues that most startups are not limited by lack of creativity, lack of talent, or lack of hard work. They are limited by how fast they can complete this feedback loop. The startup that can learn faster than its competitors will win, even if competitors have more resources.

Lesson 42: Starting the Loop - Identify What You Need to Learn

Before you build anything, identify what you most need to learn. This is the question that should drive every decision about what to build.

Ries recommends asking: what is the riskiest assumption in our current strategy? The riskiest assumption is the one that, if it turns out to be wrong, would most severely damage the business. Start there.

Design the minimum viable product that tests that assumption. Build it. Measure the results. Learn. Then move to the next most important assumption.

This approach ensures that learning is prioritized by importance rather than by what is interesting or easy to test.

Lesson 43: The Importance of Speed in the Loop

Ries emphasizes repeatedly that the speed of the build-measure-learn loop is one of the most important competitive advantages a startup can have.

The startup that runs the loop faster learns faster. Learning faster means discovering what customers actually want sooner, finding the right strategy sooner, and building the right product sooner. In a competitive environment, this speed advantage compounds over time into a significant lead.

The common mistake is to focus on making each individual loop iteration more thorough rather than making the loop faster. A startup that spends six months on each iteration, building comprehensive products and gathering extensive data, might run two iterations per year. A startup that spends two weeks on each iteration might run twenty-six iterations per year.

Even if the six-month iterations are individually more thorough, the startup running weekly iterations will generally learn more over any given time period because they can test more hypotheses, encounter more unexpected findings, and have more opportunities to pivot in productive directions.

COMPLETE SUMMARY OF ALL KEY PRINCIPLES

On the Nature of Startups and Entrepreneurship

A startup is any human institution designed to create a new product or service under conditions of extreme uncertainty.

Entrepreneurship is a management discipline, not a personality type. Managing startups effectively requires rigor and systematic thinking, not just vision and passion.

The fundamental challenge of entrepreneurship is not how to build a product. It is how to discover whether you are building the right product before investing heavily in it.

On Learning and Measurement

Validated learning is the true measure of startup progress. The question is not what did you build this month. The question is what did you learn this month.

Vanity metrics look good but do not tell you whether your business is working. Actionable metrics demonstrate clear cause and effect and tell you what to do next.

The three A's of good startup metrics: actionable, accessible, and auditable.

Cohort analysis reveals whether your product is getting better or worse over time in ways that aggregate metrics cannot.

On the Build-Measure-Learn Loop

The build-measure-learn feedback loop is the core engine of the Lean Startup. Running it faster is one of the most important competitive advantages a startup can develop.

Start every iteration by identifying what you most need to learn. Build the minimum product necessary to learn it. Measure rigorously. Learn honestly.

On the MVP

The minimum viable product is not the simplest version of the product. It is the version that collects the maximum validated learning with the minimum effort.

The purpose of the MVP is to test hypotheses, not to satisfy customers or impress investors.

Early adopters are more forgiving of product imperfection than later customers. Use early adopters to test hypotheses.

The concierge MVP, delivering the service manually before automating it, is a powerful tool for learning what customers actually need.

On the Pivot

A pivot is a structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis about the product, strategy, and engine of growth.

Most startups pivot too late because founders are emotionally invested in their original vision. Pivoting is not failure. It is the application of learning.

The decision to pivot or persevere should be driven by evidence, not by emotion or elapsed time.

There are many types of pivots: zoom-in, zoom-out, customer segment, customer need, platform, business architecture, value capture, engine of growth, channel, and technology.

On Growth

The three engines of growth are the sticky engine, the viral engine, and the paid engine.

Understanding which engine drives your business tells you which metrics matter and where to focus optimization efforts.

The sustainable engine of growth is the one that your validated learning confirms is actually driving your growth, not the one you hoped would drive it.

On Organization and Culture

Small batches are more efficient than large batches because they reveal problems earlier when they are cheaper to fix.

The Five Whys is a root cause analysis technique that reveals organizational causes of problems that are not obvious from surface symptoms.

The Five Whys requires a blameless culture to work. If the process is used to assign blame, it will stop at the first person who can be blamed rather than reaching root causes.

Adaptive organizations invest in structures that support learning and experimentation, not just structures that support efficient execution.

Innovation sandboxes allow large organizations to run lean startup experiments with real customers without creating organizational chaos.

OVERARCHING THEMES OF THE LEAN STARTUP

Theme One: Learning Is the Primary Work of a Startup

The most fundamental reframe in The Lean Startup is that the primary work of a startup is not building a product. It is learning: learning what customers want, learning what will make the business work, learning what is true about the market. Building is in service of learning.

This reframe changes everything about how you measure progress, how you make decisions, and how you organize the work of a startup.

Theme Two: Speed of Learning Is More Important Than Comprehensiveness

The competitive advantage in startup environments goes to the teams that learn fastest, not to the teams that build the most comprehensive products or gather the most comprehensive data. Running the build-measure-learn loop faster is more valuable than running it more thoroughly.

Theme Three: Assumptions Are Hypotheses to Be Tested, Not Facts to Be Assumed

Every startup is built on assumptions. The Lean Startup methodology treats these assumptions as hypotheses that must be validated by evidence rather than as facts that can be assumed. This discipline of treating assumptions as hypotheses is what distinguishes lean thinking from traditional startup thinking.

Theme Four: Waste Is Anything That Does Not Contribute to Learning

Borrowed from lean manufacturing, this principle defines waste in a way that is specific to startup environments. Features that are built but not validated are waste. Plans that are made without being tested are waste. Meetings that do not advance validated learning are waste. Eliminating this waste requires ruthless focus on what needs to be learned and what is the minimum necessary to learn it.

Theme Five: Adaptation Is Continuous, Not Occasional

The pivot is often described as a major strategic shift, which it sometimes is. But the Lean Startup methodology is really about continuous adaptation: constantly learning, constantly updating your understanding of what is true, and constantly adjusting your strategy and tactics in response to that learning. The pivot is just the most visible manifestation of this continuous adaptation.

FINAL THOUGHTS

The Lean Startup is one of the most influential business books of the past two decades. Eric Ries's methodology has been adopted by startups, large corporations, government agencies, and nonprofit organizations around the world. The concepts of the MVP, the pivot, validated learning, and the build-measure-learn loop have become part of the standard vocabulary of modern business.

The book's central insight is both simple and profound: under conditions of extreme uncertainty, the most valuable thing you can do is learn as quickly as possible whether your assumptions are correct, and you should design everything, your products, your processes, your organization, around maximizing the speed and quality of that learning.

This insight challenges the conventional wisdom about startups in fundamental ways. It challenges the idea that visionary founders should trust their gut and build their vision regardless of market feedback. It challenges the idea that the most important work of a startup is building the best possible product. It challenges the idea that traditional management approaches are appropriate for startup environments.

What Ries offers in their place is a methodology grounded in empirical reality: you do not know what customers want until you test it. You do not know what will make your business work until you experiment. The path to building something customers love and a business that works is through rapid, rigorous, honest learning from real experiments with real customers.

For anyone building something new, whether a startup, a new product within an established company, a nonprofit program, or any other venture into new territory, The Lean Startup provides the most practical and rigorous framework available for navigating the fundamental challenge of entrepreneurship: how to build something people genuinely want, without wasting time and money building something they do not.
